ESRB Rating Prediction
with K-Nearest Neighbors

Using KNN to predict the ESRB age rating of video games based on their genre, sales, platform, and critical reception.

Problem Statement

Can we predict a game's age rating from its features?

The ESRB (Entertainment Software Rating Board) assigns age ratings to video games based on their content. These ratings guide consumers and parents in making informed purchasing decisions.

This application uses K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) to classify video games into ESRB rating categories based on observable features like genre, regional sales patterns, platform family, and critical scores.

The model finds the 7 most similar games in the dataset and uses their ratings to predict the category of a new game.

ESRB Rating Categories
  • E (Everyone): Suitable for all ages
  • E10+ (Everyone 10+): Ages 10 and up
  • T (Teen): Ages 13 and up
  • M (Mature): Ages 17 and up

Methodology

Data Preparation Steps

1
Filter Valid Ratings

Keep only games with the four main ESRB ratings (E, E10+, T, M). Remove rare categories like EC, K-A, RP, and AO.

2
Clean Missing Data

Convert User_Score to numeric (removing "tbd" entries). Drop rows missing Critic_Score, User_Score, or Genre.

3
Create Platform Family Feature

Map individual platforms to families (PlayStation, Xbox, Nintendo, Other) and one-hot encode them as features.

4
Encode Categorical Variables

One-hot encode Genre (12 columns) and Platform_Family (4 columns). Encode the target Rating with LabelEncoder.

5
Scale All Features

Apply StandardScaler to normalize features. This is critical for KNN since it relies on distance calculations.

6
Train/Test Split (80/20)

Split with stratification to preserve the proportion of each rating class in both sets.

Training

Model Training Process

The model uses scikit-learn's KNeighborsClassifier with the following configuration:

  • n_neighbors={{ k_value }} — The number of nearest neighbors to consider for each prediction
  • algorithm='auto' — Automatically selects the most efficient algorithm (BallTree, KDTree, or brute-force) based on the data
  • metric='minkowski' (p=2) — Uses Euclidean distance by default

Since KNN is a lazy learner, the "training" phase simply stores the scaled training data. The actual computation happens at prediction time, when the algorithm searches for the K nearest neighbors of the new input point and takes a majority vote among their labels.

Evaluation

Confusion Matrix

How to Read the Confusion Matrix

Each row represents the actual rating and each column represents the predicted rating. The diagonal shows correct predictions.

Off-diagonal values reveal common misclassifications. For example, E10+ games are often confused with E or T games because they sit between these categories in terms of content maturity.

ROC Analysis

ROC Curves and AUC

Understanding ROC and AUC

ROC curves show the trade-off between True Positive Rate and False Positive Rate at different classification thresholds.

AUC (Area Under the Curve) summarizes performance into a single number. AUC = 1.0 means perfect classification; AUC = 0.5 is no better than random.

Each curve represents one rating vs all others (One-vs-Rest). Higher AUC values indicate that KNN can more easily distinguish that rating from the rest.

Analysis

Interpretation of Results

Genre is the strongest predictor of ESRB rating. Shooter and fighting games are strongly associated with M (Mature) ratings, while sports and puzzle games lean toward E (Everyone). This makes intuitive sense as game content correlates heavily with genre.

KNN captures non-linear patterns. Unlike logistic regression, KNN does not assume a linear relationship between features and the target. This is valuable for rating prediction, where the decision boundaries between E, E10+, T, and M are not straight lines in the feature space.

The E10+ category is the most challenging. This rating was introduced in 2005 and represents a middle ground between E and T. Games in this category share characteristics with both adjacent ratings, making classification harder. This is reflected in the lower precision and recall for E10+.

Limitations: KNN is sensitive to the scale and relevance of features. While we use StandardScaler to normalize features, some features (like sales figures) may not be strong predictors of content rating. Additionally, ESRB ratings are ultimately determined by content review, not by sales or scores, so the model is learning correlations rather than causal relationships.
