Unsupervised Learning — Clustering Application

Segmenting Video Games
by Reception Profile

A full scikit-learn K-Means pipeline applied to over 7,000 video games — discovering natural segments based on how critics and players rated them.

Context

What does the data represent?

The video game industry generates two different types of reception signal for every released title: the professional critics' opinion (Metacritic's Critic_Score) and the players' opinion (User_Score). These two signals often agree, but sometimes they diverge sharply — creating interesting market segments.

A game that critics love but players dislike, or the opposite, is a very different commercial proposition from a game that both camps praise or both camps reject. Publishers, storefronts and recommendation engines benefit from identifying these segments automatically.

This application uses K-Means clustering to discover, without any labels, how games group themselves when described only by their two reception scores.

Business Questions
  • Do critic- and user-reception patterns form natural groups?
  • How many games fall in each group?
  • What is the typical game inside each cluster?
  • Can we characterize the segments to guide marketing?

Methodology

Data Preprocessing & Model Training

1
Clean Missing Data

Convert User_Score to numeric (removing "tbd" entries) and drop rows where Critic_Score, User_Score or Global_Sales are missing. Keep only games with positive scores.

2
Feature Selection

Build the feature matrix X = [Critic_Score, User_Score]. These two numerical features describe the reception profile of each game in a directly interpretable way.

3
Feature Scaling (StandardScaler)

The two features live on different scales (0–100 vs 0–10). Apply StandardScaler to standardize them to zero mean and unit variance — essential for any distance-based algorithm like K-Means.

4
Model Training

Fit KMeans(n_clusters=3, random_state=42, n_init=10) from scikit-learn. n_init=10 runs K-Means 10 times with different seeds and keeps the best result, reducing the impact of a bad initialization.

5
Post-processing

Inverse-transform the centroids back to the original feature space so they can be interpreted in real units (critic points on 0–100, user points on 0–10).

Analysis

Interpretation of Results

K-Means identified three coherent segments that split games along the shared critic–user reception axis:

Cluster 1 — Acclaimed titles: the largest segment, with high average Critic_Score and User_Score. Critics and players agree that these games are great — the hits and evergreens that justify a recommendation push.

Cluster 2 — Solid mid-tier: the "safe bet" segment. Reception is decent but not remarkable. These games rely on genre loyalty or brand more than on buzz.

Cluster 3 — Underperformers: the smallest segment, with low scores from both camps. These titles are good candidates for deep discounts or for filtering out of featured slots.

Why K = 3 works well here: the three centroids are well separated along the critic–user diagonal, which means the clusters have clearly distinct profiles and the assignment is stable across runs.

Limitations: K-Means assumes roughly spherical clusters of similar size, which is only approximately true here. The mid-tier cluster overlaps with its neighbors at the boundaries, so games near the borders could plausibly belong to either side. Adding more features (genre, platform, sales) would refine the segmentation but also reduce visual interpretability.
